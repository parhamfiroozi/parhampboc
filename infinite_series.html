Geometric Sequences

A geometric sequence has the form $$a, ar, ar^2, ar^3, ar^4, \dots$$ where $a$ is the first term and $r$ is the common ratio. The $n$‑th term is $$a_n = a\,r^{\,n-1}.$$

Examples

  1. $3,\,3\cdot2,\,3\cdot2^2,\,3\cdot2^3,\,\dots$ — here $a=3$, $r=2$ so the sequence is $3,6,12,24,48,\dots$
  2. $2,-10,50,-250,1250,\dots$ — here $a=2$, $r=-5$.
  3. $1,\tfrac13,\tfrac19,\tfrac1{27},\dots$ — here $a=1$, $r=\tfrac13$.

Exercise

The third term of a geometric sequence is $\tfrac{63}{4}$ and the sixth term is $\tfrac{1701}{32}$. Find the fifth term.

We have $$a_3 = a\,r^{2} = \frac{63}{4},\quad a_6 = a\,r^{5} = \frac{1701}{32}.$$ Dividing gives $$\frac{a r^2}{a r^5} = \frac{63/4}{1701/32} = \frac{8}{27} = \frac{1}{r^3}$$ so $r=\tfrac{3}{2}$. From $a r^2 = \tfrac{63}{4}$ we find $a=7$. Thus $$a_5 = a r^4 = 7\bigl(\tfrac{3}{2}\bigr)^4 = \tfrac{567}{16}.$$

Partial Sums of a Geometric Series

The partial sum of the first $n$ terms of a geometric sequence is $$S_n = \sum_{k=1}^n a r^{k-1} = a + a r + a r^2 + \cdots + a r^{n-1}.$$

Finite and Infinite Geometric Series

Finite Geometric Series

If $$S_n = a + a r + a r^2 + \cdots + a r^{n-1},$$ then multiplying by $r$ and subtracting yields $$S_n = \frac{a(1-r^n)}{1-r}, \quad r \ne 1.$$

Infinite Geometric Series

The infinite series $$a + a r + a r^2 + a r^3 + \cdots$$ has partial sums $$S_n = \frac{a(1 - r^n)}{1 - r},$$ so if $|r|<1$ then $$\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} a r^k = \lim_{n\to\infty} S_n = \frac{a}{1-r},$$ while for $|r|\ge1$ it diverges.

Harmonic Series and P-Series

Harmonic Series: $$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n} \quad \text{diverges}$$

P-Series: $$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^p}\quad\text{converges for }p>1$$

Convergence Tests

Comparison Test (Limit Form)

Consider $$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{3n^2+5n}{2n^4+7}.$$ Using $b_n=1/n^2$ we compute $$\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{a_n}{b_n}=\frac32,$$ so the series converges.

Integral Test

For $$\sum_{n=2}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n\ln n}$$ compare $$\int_{2}^{\infty}\frac{dx}{x\ln x}$$ which diverges, so the series diverges.

Ratio Test

For $$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{n!}{n^n}$$ compute $$\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}=\frac1e<1,$$ hence the series converges.

D'Alembert Ratio Test: $$L=\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}.$$ If $L<1$ the series converges; if $L>1$ it diverges; if $L=1$ the test is inconclusive.

Alternating Series

An alternating series has terms that change sign. The Leibniz criterion states that $$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}(-1)^{n+1} a_n$$ converges if $a_{n+1}\le a_n$ and $\lim_{n\to\infty}a_n=0$.

Alternating and Dirichlet Tests

The same series can be shown to converge using Dirichlet's test. Write $a_n=1/n$ and $b_n=\cos(nx)$; the partial sums of $b_n$ remain bounded by $$\frac{1}{|\sin(x/2)|},$$ so the series $$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{\cos(nx)}{n}$$ converges for $x\not\equiv0\,(\text{mod }2\pi)$.

Finding Its Sum

There are many routes to $$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{\cos(nx)}{n}=-\ln\bigl(2\sin\tfrac{x}{2}\bigr).$$ One approach expands $\ln(1-z)$ for $|z|<1$ and integrates term by term. Another differentiates $$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{\cos(nx)}{n}$$ termwise to obtain $$-\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\sin(nx)= -\tfrac12\cot\bigl(\tfrac{x}{2}\bigr),$$ then integrates back.

Absolute and Conditional Convergence

An infinite series $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n$ is absolutely convergent if $\sum |a_n|$ converges. If $\sum a_n$ converges but $\sum|a_n|$ diverges, it is conditionally convergent.

Example: the alternating harmonic series $$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}(-1)^{n-1}\frac{1}{n}$$ converges, but the harmonic series diverges.

Operations on Series

  • If a series is absolutely convergent, its sum is independent of the order of addition.
  • An absolutely convergent series may be added or multiplied termwise, and the resulting series will also converge absolutely.
