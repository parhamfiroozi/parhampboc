Complex Variables

We know that: $$i^2 + 1 = 0$$

Therefore, $$i \triangleq \sqrt{-1}$$

Let a complex number be written as: $$z = x + iy$$

Then the real and imaginary parts are: $$x = \mathrm{Re}\{z\}, \quad y = \mathrm{Im}\{z\}$$

Visualized on the complex plane:

Example

Let’s divide two complex numbers:

$$\frac{3 + 4i}{1 - 2i}$$

Multiply numerator and denominator by the conjugate of the denominator:

$$ \frac{3 + 4i}{1 - 2i} \cdot \frac{1 + 2i}{1 + 2i} = \frac{(3 + 4i)(1 + 2i)}{(1 - 2i)(1 + 2i)} $$

Compute numerator:

$$ (3 + 4i)(1 + 2i) = 3 + 6i + 4i + 8i^2 = 3 + 10i - 8 = -5 + 10i $$

Compute denominator:

$$ (1 - 2i)(1 + 2i) = 1^2 - (2i)^2 = 1 - (-4) = 5 $$

Final result:

$$ \frac{-5 + 10i}{5} = -1 + 2i $$

Matrix Representation of Complex Multiplication

If we write two complex numbers as $z = x + iy$ and $w = c + id$, then their product $zw$ can be expressed using matrix multiplication:

$$ \begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} c & -d \\ d & \ \ c \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} $$

This gives us:

  • $a = cx - dy$
  • $b = dx + cy$

General Formula for Complex Division

If $z_1 = a + ib$ and $z_2 = c + id$, then the division is:

$$ \frac{a + ib}{c + id} = \frac{(a + ib)(c - id)}{(c + id)(c - id)} = \frac{ac + bd}{c^2 + d^2} + i \cdot \frac{bc - ad}{c^2 + d^2} $$

So the result is a complex number with:

  • Real part: $\frac{ac + bd}{c^2 + d^2}$
  • Imaginary part: $\frac{bc - ad}{c^2 + d^2}$

Complex Conjugate

The complex conjugate of a complex number $z = x + iy$ is denoted by $\bar{z}$ and is defined as:

$$ \bar{z} = x - iy $$

This reflects the complex number across the real axis in the complex plane.

Example

If $z = 3 + 4i$, then its conjugate is:

$$ \bar{z} = 3 - 4i $$

Multiplying a complex number by its conjugate gives a real number:

$$ z \cdot \bar{z} = (3 + 4i)(3 - 4i) = 9 + 16 = 25 $$

Polar Representation of Complex Numbers

Imagine you're on a map. You're starting at the center (like the number 0). If someone says:

"Walk 5 steps at an angle of 45 degrees", then:

  • 5 is how far you go — that's the magnitude (or r).
  • 45° is the direction — that's the angle (or θ).

This new spot on the map is a complex number. Instead of saying x + iy, we say:

$$ z = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta) $$

Or using Euler’s beautiful shortcut:

$$ z = re^{i\theta} $$

Example

If you go 1 step straight up (that’s 90°), then:

  • r = 1
  • θ = 90° or π/2 radians

$$ z = 1(\cos\frac{\pi}{2} + i\sin\frac{\pi}{2}) = 0 + i(1) = i $$

So, i is just one step straight up from 0.

Euler's Formula via Taylor Series

Euler's formula is a beautiful and fundamental identity in complex analysis, given by:

$$ e^{ix} = \cos x + i \sin x $$

There are several ways to prove it, but one of the cleanest methods is via Taylor series expansion. Here's the proof using that approach.

Step-by-step Proof using Taylor Series

1. Taylor series for \( e^{ix} \), \( \cos x \), and \( \sin x \)

We use the Maclaurin (Taylor at 0) series expansions:

  • Exponential: $$ e^{ix} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(ix)^n}{n!} $$
  • Cosine: $$ \cos x = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n}}{(2n)!} $$
  • Sine: $$ \sin x = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!} $$
2. Expand \( e^{ix} \)

Let’s compute the terms of \( e^{ix} \):

$$ e^{ix} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(ix)^n}{n!} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{i^n x^n}{n!} $$

Now split the sum into even and odd powers:

  • Even \( n = 2k \): \( i^{2k} = (-1)^k \)
  • Odd \( n = 2k+1 \): \( i^{2k+1} = i \cdot (-1)^k \)

So:

$$ e^{ix} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^k x^{2k}}{(2k)!} + i \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^k x^{2k+1}}{(2k+1)!} $$

3. Recognize the series
  • The first sum is exactly \( \cos x \)
  • The second sum is \( \sin x \)

Thus:

$$ e^{ix} = \cos x + i \sin x $$

Conclusion

$$ \boxed{e^{ix} = \cos x + i \sin x} $$

This identity works for all real \( x \), and it's the cornerstone of many beautiful results in math and engineering — including Euler's identity:

$$ e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0 $$

Computing the Argument (Angle) of a Complex Number

Given a complex number \( z = x + iy \), we want to compute the angle \( \theta \) that the number makes with the positive real axis. This angle is also called the argument of the complex number.

Step-by-Step Method

  1. Identify real and imaginary parts:
    From \( z = x + iy \), take:
        \( x = \mathrm{Re}(z) \)
        \( y = \mathrm{Im}(z) \)
  2. Use the function:
    $$ \theta = \arg(z) = \mathrm{atan2}(y, x) $$ This version of arctangent correctly handles all quadrants and division by zero.
  3. Convert to degrees (if needed):
    $$ \theta_{\text{deg}} = \theta \cdot \frac{180}{\pi} $$

Why atan2 is Better Than \tan^{-1}(y/x)

  • It handles all four quadrants correctly.
  • It avoids division by zero when \( x = 0 \).
  • It returns an angle in radians in the range \( (-\pi, \pi] \).

Examples

Example 1: \( z = 1 + i \)

$$ \theta = \mathrm{atan2}(1, 1) = \frac{\pi}{4} $$

Example 2: \( z = -1 - \sqrt{3}i \)

$$ \theta = \mathrm{atan2}(-\sqrt{3}, -1) = -\frac{2\pi}{3} $$

Visualization Tip

Think of \( z = x + iy \) as a point \( (x, y) \) on the plane. The angle \( \theta \) is the direction of the vector from the origin to that point.

Complex Number Operations Using r cos(θ) and r sin(θ)

Let a complex number be written as:

$$ z = r \cos\theta + i r \sin\theta $$

Addition

If \( z_1 = r_1 \cos\theta_1 + i r_1 \sin\theta_1 \) and \( z_2 = r_2 \cos\theta_2 + i r_2 \sin\theta_2 \), then:

$$ z_1 + z_2 = (r_1 \cos\theta_1 + r_2 \cos\theta_2) + i(r_1 \sin\theta_1 + r_2 \sin\theta_2) $$

Note: You must compute each real and imaginary part separately — the result is no longer in polar form.

Multiplication

If \( z_1 = r_1(\cos\theta_1 + i\sin\theta_1) \) and \( z_2 = r_2(\cos\theta_2 + i\sin\theta_2) \), then use:

$$ z_1 z_2 = r_1 r_2 [\cos(\theta_1 + \theta_2) + i\sin(\theta_1 + \theta_2)] $$

Expanding in terms of r cos(θ) and r sin(θ) gives:

$$ z_1 z_2 = r_1 r_2 \cos(\theta_1 + \theta_2) + i r_1 r_2 \sin(\theta_1 + \theta_2) $$

Division

If \( z_1 = r_1(\cos\theta_1 + i\sin\theta_1) \), \( z_2 = r_2(\cos\theta_2 + i\sin\theta_2) \), then:

$$ \frac{z_1}{z_2} = \frac{r_1}{r_2} [\cos(\theta_1 - \theta_2) + i\sin(\theta_1 - \theta_2)] $$

Which becomes:

$$ \frac{z_1}{z_2} = \frac{r_1}{r_2} \cos(\theta_1 - \theta_2) + i \frac{r_1}{r_2} \sin(\theta_1 - \theta_2) $$

This keeps everything in the same r cos(θ) + i r sin(θ) format.

Example: Multiplying Complex Numbers Using Polar Form

Given:

  • \( z_1 = -2 \)
  • \( z_2 = -2 - 2i \)

Step 1: Convert to Polar Form

For \( z_1 = -2 \):

  • Magnitude: \( r_1 = |z_1| = 2 \)
  • Angle: \( \theta_1 = \pi \) (lies on the negative real axis)

$$ z_1 = 2\cos(\pi) + i \cdot 2\sin(\pi) = -2 + 0i $$

For \( z_2 = -2 - 2i \):

  • Magnitude: \( r_2 = \sqrt{(-2)^2 + (-2)^2} = \sqrt{8} = 2\sqrt{2} \)
  • Angle:
    \( \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{-2}{-2}\right) = \frac{\pi}{4} \) (but both components are negative ⇒ 3rd quadrant)
    \( \theta_2 = \pi + \frac{\pi}{4} = \frac{5\pi}{4} \)

$$ z_2 = 2\sqrt{2} \cos\left(\frac{5\pi}{4}\right) + i \cdot 2\sqrt{2} \sin\left(\frac{5\pi}{4}\right) $$

Step 2: Multiply in Polar Form

$$ z_1 z_2 = r_1 r_2 \left[\cos(\theta_1 + \theta_2) + i \sin(\theta_1 + \theta_2)\right] $$

Substituting:

  • \( r_1 = 2 \), \( r_2 = 2\sqrt{2} \)
  • \( \theta_1 + \theta_2 = \pi + \frac{5\pi}{4} = \frac{9\pi}{4} \)
  • \( \frac{9\pi}{4} = \frac{\pi}{4} \) (since angles are periodic every \( 2\pi \))

$$ z_1 z_2 = 4\sqrt{2} \left[\cos\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right) + i \sin\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right)\right] = 4\sqrt{2} \left( \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} + i \cdot \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \right) $$

$$ = 4(1 + i) = 4 + 4i $$

Final Answer:

$$ z_1 z_2 = 4 + 4i $$

Multiplication of Complex Numbers in Polar Form

Suppose we have:

$$ z = z_1 \cdot z_2 \cdot \ldots \cdot z_n $$

Each complex number in polar form:

$$ z_i = (r_i, \theta_i) $$

Then:

  • $$ |z| = r_1 \cdot r_2 \cdot \ldots \cdot r_n $$
  • $$ \arg(z) = \sum_{i=1}^n \arg(z_i) + k2\pi $$

Special Case: Power of a Complex Number

Let:

$$ z_1 = z_2 = \ldots = z_n = z', \quad z' = (r', \theta') $$

Then:

  • $$ z = z'^n, \quad n \in \mathbb{N} $$
  • $$ r = (r')^n $$
  • $$ |z^n| = |z_1|^n $$
  • $$ \arg(z) = n\theta' + k2\pi $$

Example: Special Case — Raising a Complex Number to a Power

Let:

$$ z' = 2 \left( \cos\left( \frac{\pi}{3} \right) + i \sin\left( \frac{\pi}{3} \right) \right) $$

We want to compute:

$$ z = (z')^3 $$

  • Magnitude: \( r = 2^3 = 8 \)
  • Angle: \( \theta = 3 \cdot \frac{\pi}{3} = \pi \)

So:

$$ z = 8 \left( \cos(\pi) + i \sin(\pi) \right) = 8(-1 + 0i) = -8 $$

What Does the Prime (′) Mean?

The prime symbol (′) in this context does not mean derivative. It’s simply a label used to refer to a base complex number and its parts.

Specifically:

  • \( z' \): the base complex number (which is being raised to a power)
  • \( r' \): the magnitude (modulus) of \( z' \)
  • \( \theta' \): the angle (argument) of \( z' \)

So when we write something like:

$$ z = (z')^n $$

We mean that the complex number \( z' \) is multiplied by itself \( n \) times, and its polar form is used to compute the result efficiently using:

  • Magnitude: \( r = (r')^n \)
  • Angle: \( \theta = n\theta' \)

This is a standard technique in polar form, and is closely tied to De Moivre's Theorem.

Division of Complex Numbers in Polar Form

General Formula

Let:

$$ z_1 = r_1 (\cos\theta_1 + i\sin\theta_1), \quad z_2 = r_2 (\cos\theta_2 + i\sin\theta_2) $$

Then:

$$ \frac{z_1}{z_2} = \frac{r_1}{r_2} \left[ \cos(\theta_1 - \theta_2) + i\sin(\theta_1 - \theta_2) \right] $$

Special Case: Repeated Division

If all complex numbers are the same \( z' \), then dividing \( z' \) by itself \( n \) times gives:

$$ z = (z')^{-n}, \quad z' = (r', \theta') $$

  • Magnitude: \( r = (r')^{-n} \)
  • Angle: \( \theta = -n\theta' + k2\pi \)

Meaning of the Prime (′)

The prime (′) indicates the base complex number. It is used to distinguish that all the values being divided are the same:

  • \( z' \): the base complex number
  • \( r' \): its magnitude
  • \( \theta' \): its argument (angle)

No derivative is involved — it's just labeling.

Example: Division of a Complex Number Raised to a Negative Power

Let:

$$ z' = 2 \left( \cos\left( \frac{\pi}{6} \right) + i\sin\left( \frac{\pi}{6} \right) \right) $$

Find:

$$ z = (z')^{-2} $$

  • Magnitude: \( r = 2^{-2} = \frac{1}{4} \)
  • Angle: \( \theta = -2 \cdot \frac{\pi}{6} = -\frac{\pi}{3} \)

So:

$$ z = \frac{1}{4} \left( \cos\left(-\frac{\pi}{3}\right) + i\sin\left(-\frac{\pi}{3}\right) \right) = \frac{1}{4} \left( \frac{1}{2} - i\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \right) = \frac{1}{8} - i\frac{\sqrt{3}}{8} $$

General Expression for \( \cos(5\theta) \) Using De Moivre’s Theorem

Start from:

$$ (\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)^5 = \cos(5\theta) + i\sin(5\theta) $$

Apply binomial expansion to the left-hand side:

$$ \sum_{k=0}^5 \binom{5}{k} \cos^{5-k} \theta \cdot (i \sin\theta)^k $$

Extract the real part (even powers of \( i \)):

$$ \cos(5\theta) = \cos^5\theta - 10\cos^3\theta\sin^2\theta + 5\cos\theta\sin^4\theta $$

Finding the \( n \)th Root of a Complex Number

Let a complex number be written in polar form:

$$ z = r\left( \cos\theta + i\sin\theta \right) $$

The \( n \)th roots of \( z \) are given by the formula:

$$ z_k = \sqrt[n]{r} \left[ \cos\left( \frac{\theta + 2k\pi}{n} \right) + i \sin\left( \frac{\theta + 2k\pi}{n} \right) \right] $$

Where:

  • \( k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots, n-1 \)
  • \( r \) is the modulus (absolute value) of the complex number
  • \( \theta \) is the argument (angle)

Key Ideas:

  • You take the \( n \)th root of the magnitude
  • You divide the angle by \( n \) and rotate by \( \frac{2\pi}{n} \) for each root
  • The roots are equally spaced on a circle in the complex plane

Example: Finding the 4th Roots of 1 and -1

1. 4th Roots of 1

Write in polar form:

$$ 1 = 1(\cos 0 + i\sin 0) $$

Use the formula:

$$ z_k = \sqrt[4]{1} \left[ \cos\left( \frac{2k\pi}{4} \right) + i\sin\left( \frac{2k\pi}{4} \right) \right] $$

Roots:

  • \( z_0 = 1 \)
  • \( z_1 = i \)
  • \( z_2 = -1 \)
  • \( z_3 = -i \)

Answer: \( \boxed{1,\ i,\ -1,\ -i} \)

2. 4th Roots of -1

Write in polar form:

$$ -1 = 1(\cos \pi + i \sin \pi) $$

Use the formula:

$$ z_k = \sqrt[4]{1} \left[ \cos\left( \frac{\pi + 2k\pi}{4} \right) + i\sin\left( \frac{\pi + 2k\pi}{4} \right) \right] $$

Roots:

  • \( z_0 = \cos\frac{\pi}{4} + i\sin\frac{\pi}{4} \)
  • \( z_1 = \cos\frac{3\pi}{4} + i\sin\frac{3\pi}{4} \)
  • \( z_2 = \cos\frac{5\pi}{4} + i\sin\frac{5\pi}{4} \)
  • \( z_3 = \cos\frac{7\pi}{4} + i\sin\frac{7\pi}{4} \)

Answer:

$$ \boxed{ \cos\frac{\pi}{4} + i\sin\frac{\pi}{4},\ \cos\frac{3\pi}{4} + i\sin\frac{3\pi}{4},\ \cos\frac{5\pi}{4} + i\sin\frac{5\pi}{4},\ \cos\frac{7\pi}{4} + i\sin\frac{7\pi}{4} } $$

Computing Fractional Powers of Complex Numbers: \( z^{p/q} \)

Let \( z \) be written in polar form:

$$ z = r \left( \cos \theta + i \sin \theta \right) $$

Then for rational exponents \( \frac{p}{q} \), the complex number raised to that power is:

$$ z^{p/q} = r^{p/q} \left[ \cos\left( \frac{p\theta + 2\pi pk}{q} \right) + i \sin\left( \frac{p\theta + 2\pi pk}{q} \right) \right] $$

Where:

  • \( p, q \in \mathbb{Z} \), and \( q > 0 \)
  • \( k = 0, 1, \dots, q - 1 \) gives the q distinct values (branches)

What It Means:

  • You're computing both a root and a power at once
  • Multiple values are possible due to the periodic nature of complex angles

Example: Compute \( (-1 + i)^{2/3} \)

Step 1: Convert to Polar Form

$$ z = -1 + i = \sqrt{2} \left( \cos\frac{3\pi}{4} + i \sin\frac{3\pi}{4} \right) $$

Step 2: Use the Formula

$$ z^{2/3} = 2^{1/3} \left[ \cos\left( \frac{2\cdot\frac{3\pi}{4} + 4k\pi}{3} \right) + i \sin\left( \frac{2\cdot\frac{3\pi}{4} + 4k\pi}{3} \right) \right] $$

Roots:

  • \( k = 0 \):
    \( \theta = \frac{\pi}{2} \Rightarrow z_0 = 2^{1/3} \cdot i \)
  • \( k = 1 \):
    \( \theta = \frac{11\pi}{6} \Rightarrow z_1 = 2^{1/3} \left( \cos\frac{11\pi}{6} + i\sin\frac{11\pi}{6} \right) \)
  • \( k = 2 \):
    \( \theta = \frac{19\pi}{6} \Rightarrow z_2 = 2^{1/3} \left( \cos\frac{19\pi}{6} + i\sin\frac{19\pi}{6} \right) \)

Final Answer:

$$ \boxed{ (-1 + i)^{2/3} = \left\{ 2^{1/3} \cdot i,\ 2^{1/3} \left( \cos\frac{11\pi}{6} + i\sin\frac{11\pi}{6} \right),\ 2^{1/3} \left( \cos\frac{7\pi}{6} + i\sin\frac{7\pi}{6} \right) \right\} } $$

Modulus and Inequality Properties of Complex Numbers

Let \( z = x + iy \), where:

  • \( x = \text{Re}(z) \)
  • \( y = \text{Im}(z) \)
  • \( |z| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2} \)

Useful Inequalities

  1. Modulus vs. Real Part:
    $$ |z| \geq |\text{Re}(z)| $$
  2. Modulus vs. Imaginary Part:
    $$ |z| \geq |\text{Im}(z)| $$
  3. Triangle Inequality:
    $$ |z_1 + z_2| \leq |z_1| + |z_2| $$
  4. Reverse Triangle Inequality:
    $$ \left||z_1| - |z_2|\right| \leq |z_1 - z_2| $$

What Is a Complex Function?

A complex function is a rule that assigns a complex number to another complex number.

It maps: $$ f : \mathbb{C} \to \mathbb{C} $$

That means: it takes a complex number \( z \) as input and gives a complex number \( w \) as output.

If: $$ z = x + iy $$ Then: $$ f(z) = w \in \mathbb{C} $$

This is just like functions from real numbers, but now using complex numbers.

Complex Function Example

Let the complex number be:

$$ z = x + iy $$

Define a complex function \( f \) such that:

$$ f(z) = w = x^2 + ixy $$

This function takes the real and imaginary parts of \( z \) and returns a complex number \( w \).

We can separate this into real and imaginary parts:

$$ f(z) = u(x, y) + i v(x, y) $$ where: $$ u(x, y) = x^2,\quad v(x, y) = xy $$

Evaluating a Complex Function

Let:

$$ z = x + iy,\quad f(z) = w = x^4 + ixy $$

Examples

  • \( f(i) \):
    \( x = 0,\ y = 1 \Rightarrow w = 0 + i(0)(1) = \boxed{0} \)
  • \( f(-1 + i) \):
    \( x = -1,\ y = 1 \Rightarrow w = 1 + i(-1)(1) = \boxed{1 - i} \)
  • \( f(1) \):
    \( x = 1,\ y = 0 \Rightarrow w = 1^4 + i(1)(0) = \boxed{1} \)

Neighbourhood in Complex Analysis

A neighbourhood of a complex number \( z_0 \) is the set of all complex numbers within a certain distance from \( z_0 \).

Formally, for any \( \varepsilon > 0 \), the neighbourhood of \( z_0 \) is:

$$ N_\varepsilon(z_0) = \{ z \in \mathbb{C} \mid |z - z_0| < \varepsilon \} $$

This describes a disk centered at \( z_0 \), with radius \( \varepsilon \), and includes all points inside (not the edge).

Punctured Neighbourhood

If we exclude the center point \( z_0 \), we get a punctured neighbourhood:

$$ N^*_\varepsilon(z_0) = \{ z \in \mathbb{C} \mid 0 < |z - z_0| < \varepsilon \} $$

This is used when we want to look around a point, but not include the point itself.

Neighbourhood as a Disk in \( \mathbb{C} \)

Let: $$ z = x + iy, \quad z_0 = x_0 + iy_0 $$

Then the modulus: $$ |z - z_0| = |(x - x_0) + i(y - y_0)| = \sqrt{(x - x_0)^2 + (y - y_0)^2} $$

So the neighbourhood of radius \( \varepsilon \) around \( z_0 \) is:

$$ |z - z_0| < \varepsilon \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad (x - x_0)^2 + (y - y_0)^2 < \varepsilon^2 $$

This defines a circular region (open disk) centered at \( z_0 \) in the complex plane.

Inner (Interior) Points of a Set

Let \( S \subseteq \mathbb{C} \) be a set of complex numbers.

A point \( z_0 \in S \) is called an inner point (or interior point) if there exists a neighbourhood around it that lies completely inside the set:

$$ \exists\ \varepsilon > 0 \quad \text{such that} \quad N_\varepsilon(z_0) \subseteq S $$

Where the neighbourhood is:

$$ N_\varepsilon(z_0) = \{ z \in \mathbb{C} \mid |z - z_0| < \varepsilon \} $$

This means you can draw a small open disk around \( z_0 \) and stay entirely inside the set \( S \).

Outer (Exterior) Points of a Set

Let \( S \subseteq \mathbb{C} \) be a set in the complex plane.

A point \( z_0 \in \mathbb{C} \) is called an outer point (or exterior point) of the set if there exists a neighbourhood around \( z_0 \) that lies completely outside of \( S \):

$$ \exists\ \varepsilon > 0 \quad \text{such that} \quad N_\varepsilon(z_0) \subseteq \mathbb{C} \setminus S $$

Where the neighbourhood is:

$$ N_\varepsilon(z_0) = \{ z \in \mathbb{C} \mid |z - z_0| < \varepsilon \} $$

This means the point \( z_0 \) and all nearby points are not in the set — they lie entirely in the complement of \( S \).

Boundary Points of a Set

Let \( S \subseteq \mathbb{C} \) be a set.

A point \( z_0 \in \mathbb{C} \) is called a boundary point of \( S \) if every neighbourhood around it contains both points in \( S \) and points not in \( S \):

$$ \forall\ \varepsilon > 0,\quad N_\varepsilon(z_0) \cap S \neq \emptyset \quad \text{and} \quad N_\varepsilon(z_0) \cap (\mathbb{C} \setminus S) \neq \emptyset $$

Simply Connected Sets

A set \( S \subseteq \mathbb{C} \) is called simply connected if:

  • It is connected: any two points in the set can be joined by a continuous path that lies entirely within the set.
  • It has no holes: any closed curve in the set can be continuously contracted to a point, staying inside the set.

Important:

"Simply connected" is a property of sets, not individual points. A point can lie inside a simply connected set, but a single point is not described as simply connected.

Examples:

This means you can’t fully stay inside or outside the set when you zoom in around the boundary point — it always touches both sides.

Set Simply Connected? Reason
\( \mathbb{C} \) Yes Whole complex plane, no holes
\( \{ z \mid |z| < 1 \} \) Yes Open disk, no holes
\( \{ z \mid 1 < |z| < 2 \} \) No Annulus, hole in the middle
\( \mathbb{C} \setminus \{0\} \) No Hole at the origin

Types of Points in a Set

Type of Point Definition
Interior point Has a neighbourhood fully contained in the set
Exterior point Has a neighbourhood fully outside the set
Boundary point Every neighbourhood intersects both the set and its complement
Accumulation point Every neighbourhood contains other points from the set
Isolated point Neighbourhood contains no other points from the set
Limit point Same as accumulation point
Adherent point Every neighbourhood contains at least one point from the set
Cluster point Same as accumulation point

Limit of a Complex Function

Let \( f(z) \) be a complex function. We say that:

$$ \lim_{z \to z_0} f(z) = L $$

if \( f(z) \) approaches the complex number \( L \) as \( z \) approaches \( z_0 \) from any direction in the complex plane.

Formal Definition:

$$ \forall\ \varepsilon > 0,\ \exists\ \delta > 0\ \text{ such that if } 0 < |z - z_0| < \delta,\ \text{ then } |f(z) - L| < \varepsilon $$

Key Idea:

  • The value of \( f(z) \) gets arbitrarily close to \( L \)
  • As \( z \) gets arbitrarily close to \( z_0 \)
  • This must happen from all possible directions in the complex plane

Continuity of a Complex Function

A complex function \( f(z) \) is continuous at a point \( z_0 \in \mathbb{C} \) if:

$$ \lim_{z \to z_0} f(z) = f(z_0) $$

This means:

  1. The function is defined at \( z_0 \)
  2. The limit \( \lim_{z \to z_0} f(z) \) exists
  3. The limit equals the value: \( \lim_{z \to z_0} f(z) = f(z_0) \)

Continuity on a Set:

A function \( f(z) \) is continuous on a set \( S \subseteq \mathbb{C} \) if it is continuous at every point in \( S \).

Example: \( f(z) = \frac{1}{z} \)

1. Domain

$$ \text{Domain} = \mathbb{C} \setminus \{0\} $$

This function is defined for all complex numbers except \( z = 0 \).

2. Continuity

  • Continuous on the entire domain \( \mathbb{C} \setminus \{0\} \)
  • Not continuous at \( z = 0 \) because it is not defined there

Conclusion:

$$ f(z) = \frac{1}{z} \text{ is continuous on } \mathbb{C} \setminus \{0\} $$

Differentiation of Complex Functions

A complex function \( f(z) \) is differentiable at a point \( z_0 \in \mathbb{C} \) if the following limit exists:

$$ f'(z_0) = \lim_{z \to z_0} \frac{f(z) - f(z_0)}{z - z_0} $$

This is called the complex derivative of \( f \) at \( z_0 \).

Key Idea:

  • The limit must exist and give the same value no matter which direction \( z \) approaches \( z_0 \).
  • This is a stronger condition than in real calculus.

Note:

If a function is differentiable at every point in an open set, it is called analytic (or holomorphic) on that set.

Differentiability of \( f(z) = \overline{z} \) Using Limits

To check differentiability at a point \( z_0 \in \mathbb{C} \), we use:

$$ f'(z_0) = \lim_{z \to z_0} \frac{f(z) - f(z_0)}{z - z_0} $$

Step 1: Along the Real Axis

Let \( z = z_0 + h \), \( h \in \mathbb{R} \):

$$ \frac{\overline{z_0 + h} - \overline{z_0}}{h} = \frac{\overline{h}}{h} = \frac{h}{h} = 1 $$

Step 2: Along the Imaginary Axis

Let \( z = z_0 + ih \), \( h \in \mathbb{R} \):

$$ \frac{\overline{z_0 + ih} - \overline{z_0}}{ih} = \frac{-i h}{i h} = -1 $$

Conclusion:

The limit depends on the direction: $$ \Rightarrow \text{Limit does not exist} \Rightarrow f(z) = \overline{z} \text{ is not differentiable at any point} $$

Differentiation via Limits and the Cauchy-Riemann Equations

Let \( f(z) = u(x, y) + i v(x, y) \), with \( z = x + iy \).

We compute the derivative at \( z_0 = x_0 + iy_0 \) using limits from two paths:

Path 1: Real Direction

Let \( z = z_0 + h \), \( h \in \mathbb{R} \):

$$ \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(z_0 + h) - f(z_0)}{h} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + i \frac{\partial v}{\partial x} $$

Path 2: Imaginary Direction

Let \( z = z_0 + ih \), \( h \in \mathbb{R} \):

$$ \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(z_0 + ih) - f(z_0)}{ih} = \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} - i \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} $$

Equating Limits:

$$ \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + i \frac{\partial v}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} - i \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} $$

Matching real and imaginary parts gives the Cauchy-Riemann equations:

$$ \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial v}{\partial y},\quad \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = -\frac{\partial v}{\partial x} $$

Conclusion:

If these conditions are satisfied and the partial derivatives are continuous, then \( f(z) \) is differentiable at \( z_0 \).

Differentiability and the Cauchy-Riemann Equations

1. Definition of Differentiability

A complex function \( f(z) \) is differentiable at \( z_0 \in \mathbb{C} \) if:

$$ \lim_{z \to z_0} \frac{f(z) - f(z_0)}{z - z_0} \text{ exists and is finite} $$

2. Cauchy-Riemann Equations

Write \( f(z) = u(x, y) + i v(x, y) \), where \( z = x + iy \). Then:

$$ \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial v}{\partial y},\quad \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = -\frac{\partial v}{\partial x} $$

3. Role of Cauchy-Riemann

  • If these equations are not satisfied, \( f(z) \) is not differentiable at that point.
  • If they are satisfied and partial derivatives are continuous, then \( f(z) \) is differentiable.

4. Summary:

Condition Result
Cauchy-Riemann fails Not differentiable
Cauchy-Riemann holds, partials not continuous Might not be differentiable
Cauchy-Riemann holds, partials continuous Differentiable

Comparison: \( f(z) = z^2 \) vs \( f(z) = |z|^2 \)

1. \( f(z) = z^2 \)

$$ z = x + iy \Rightarrow f(z) = x^2 - y^2 + 2ixy $$ $$ u(x, y) = x^2 - y^2,\quad v(x, y) = 2xy $$

Partial derivatives:

$$ \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = 2x,\quad \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} = 2x \\ \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = -2y,\quad \frac{\partial v}{\partial x} = 2y $$

Cauchy-Riemann equations are satisfied everywhere.

Conclusion: \( f(z) = z^2 \) is differentiable and analytic on \( \mathbb{C} \).

2. \( f(z) = |z|^2 \)

$$ f(z) = |z|^2 = x^2 + y^2,\quad u(x, y) = x^2 + y^2,\quad v(x, y) = 0 $$

Partial derivatives:

$$ \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = 2x,\quad \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} = 0 \\ \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = 2y,\quad \frac{\partial v}{\partial x} = 0 $$

Cauchy-Riemann equations are not satisfied.

Conclusion: \( f(z) = |z|^2 \) is not differentiable anywhere.

Differentiability of \( f(z) = |z|^2 \)

1. Using the Definition of the Complex Derivative

We compute the derivative at \( z = 0 \):

$$ f'(0) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(h) - f(0)}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{|h|^2}{h} $$

Let \( h = r e^{i\theta} \). Then: $$ \frac{|h|^2}{h} = \frac{r^2}{r e^{i\theta}} = r e^{-i\theta} $$

Since \( |r e^{-i\theta}| = r \to 0 \), the whole expression tends to 0. So: $$ \boxed{f'(0) = 0} $$

2. Using the Cauchy–Riemann Equations

Write: $$ f(z) = |z|^2 = x^2 + y^2 = u(x, y) + i v(x, y) $$ with \( u(x, y) = x^2 + y^2 \), \( v(x, y) = 0 \).

Partial derivatives:

$$ u_x = 2x,\quad u_y = 2y,\quad v_x = 0,\quad v_y = 0 $$

At \( (x, y) = (0, 0) \):

$$ u_x = v_y = 0,\quad u_y = -v_x = 0 $$

The Cauchy–Riemann equations are satisfied and partial derivatives are continuous, so the function is differentiable at \( z = 0 \), with:

$$ f'(0) = u_x(0, 0) + i v_x(0, 0) = 0 $$

3. Not Differentiable Elsewhere

For \( z_0 \ne 0 \), approach along two paths:

$$ \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{|z_0 + h|^2 - |z_0|^2}{h} \quad \text{vs.} \quad \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{|z_0 + i h|^2 - |z_0|^2}{i h} $$

The first gives \( 2\operatorname{Re}(z_0) \), the second gives \( 2i\operatorname{Im}(z_0) \) ⟹ different values.

Therefore, \( f \) is not differentiable at any \( z_0 \ne 0 \).

Summary

  • \( f(z) = |z|^2 \) is complex-differentiable at \( z = 0 \), and \( f'(0) = 0 \)
  • It is not differentiable anywhere else
  • So \( f \) is not analytic (holomorphic) on any open set

Proof that the function is not differentiable at \(z=0\)

For \(z\neq0\) define

\[ f(z)=\frac{x^{3}-y^{3}}{x^{2}+y^{2}} \;+\; i\,\frac{x^{3}+y^{3}}{x^{2}+y^{2}}, \qquad z=x+iy, \qquad f(0)=0. \]

We test the complex derivative \(f'(0)\) by computing

\[ \lim_{z\to0}\frac{f(z)-f(0)}{z}\;=\; \lim_{z\to0}\frac{f(z)}{z}, \qquad z\neq0. \]

Path toward 0 Difference quotient Limit value
Real axis \((z=h,\;h\in\mathbb R)\) \(\displaystyle \frac{f(h)}{h} =\frac{h+i\,h}{h}=1+i \) \(1+i\)
Imaginary axis \((z=ih)\) \(\displaystyle \frac{f(ih)}{ih} =\frac{-h+i\,h}{i\,h}=1+i \) \(1+i\)
Diagonal \(y=x\) \((z=x+ix)\) \(\displaystyle \frac{f(x+ix)}{x(1+i)} =\frac{i\,x}{x(1+i)} =\frac{1+i}{2} \) \(\dfrac{1+i}{2}\)

The three directional limits are not the same. Hence the limit \(\displaystyle \lim_{z\to0}\tfrac{f(z)}{z}\) does not exist, so the complex derivative cannot exist at the origin.

\(f\) is not complex‑differentiable at \(z=0\).

Directional Limit Test for Differentiability at \( z = 0 \)

Let:

\( f(z) = \dfrac{x^3 - y^3}{x^2 + y^2} + i \cdot \dfrac{x^3 + y^3}{x^2 + y^2}, \quad z = x + iy, \quad f(0) = 0 \)

We compute the limit of:

\[ \lim_{z \to 0} \frac{f(z)}{z} \]

along different paths to determine if it exists.

Path 1: Along the Real Axis \( (y = 0) \)

  • \( f(z) = h + ih \)
  • \( \frac{f(h)}{h} = 1 + i \)

Path 2: Along the Imaginary Axis \( (x = 0) \)

  • \( f(z) = -h + ih \)
  • \( \frac{f(ih)}{ih} = \frac{-1 + i}{i} = 1 + i \)

Path 3: Along the Line \( y = x \)

  • \( f(z) = i x \)
  • \( z = x(1 + i) \Rightarrow \frac{f(z)}{z} = \frac{i}{1 + i} = \frac{1 + i}{2} \)

Conclusion:

The limit depends on the path:

  • Real axis: \( 1 + i \)
  • Imaginary axis: \( 1 + i \)
  • Diagonal \( y = x \): \( \dfrac{1 + i}{2} \)

Therefore, the limit \(\displaystyle \lim_{z \to 0} \frac{f(z)}{z} \) does not exist.

\( f(z) \) is not complex-differentiable at \( z = 0 \)

Analytic (Analytical) Functions in Complex Analysis

A complex function \( f(z) \) is called analytic at a point \( z_0 \) if it is differentiable in an open neighborhood around that point.

Formally:

$$ f \text{ is analytic at } z_0 \iff \exists\ \varepsilon > 0 \text{ such that } f \text{ is differentiable for all } z \text{ with } |z - z_0| < \varepsilon $$

Key Requirements:

  • Function must be complex-differentiable at all points in some disk around \( z_0 \)
  • Cauchy–Riemann equations must hold and partials must be continuous

Examples:

Function Analytic Where?
\( f(z) = z^n \) Everywhere in \( \mathbb{C} \)
\( f(z) = \frac{1}{z} \) \( \mathbb{C} \setminus \{0\} \)
\( f(z) = \overline{z} \) Nowhere analytic
\( f(z) = |z|^2 \) Not analytic anywhere

Singular Points in Complex Analysis

A point \( z_0 \in \mathbb{C} \) is called a singular point of a function \( f(z) \) if:

  • \( f(z) \) is not analytic at \( z_0 \),
  • but is analytic in some neighborhood around \( z_0 \) (excluding the point).

Example:

\( f(z) = \frac{1}{z} \) has a singularity at \( z = 0 \), because it is analytic on \( \mathbb{C} \setminus \{0\} \).

Types of Isolated Singularities:

Type Definition Example
Removable Limit exists; function can be redefined to be analytic \( \frac{\sin z}{z} \) at \( z = 0 \)
Pole Function goes to infinity as \( z \to z_0 \) \( \frac{1}{z} \), \( \frac{1}{z^2} \)
Essential Limit does not exist; behavior is chaotic \( e^{1/z} \), \( \sin(1/z) \)

Non-Isolated Singularities:

These are not alone — they cluster. Example: \( f(z) = \frac{1}{\sin(1/z)} \) near \( z = 0 \).

The Laplace Equation in Complex Analysis

The Laplace equation is a second-order PDE given by:

$$ \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2} = 0 $$

A function \( u(x, y) \) that satisfies this equation is called harmonic.

Key Fact:

If \( f(z) = u(x, y) + i v(x, y) \) is analytic, then both \( u \) and \( v \) satisfy the Laplace equation.

Example:

Let \( f(z) = z^2 = x^2 - y^2 + i(2xy) \). Then:

  • \( u(x, y) = x^2 - y^2 \), \( \Delta u = 2 - 2 = 0 \)
  • \( v(x, y) = 2xy \),    \( \Delta v = 0 + 0 = 0 \)

Both parts are harmonic.

Conclusion:

All analytic functions have harmonic real and imaginary parts, and thus satisfy the Laplace equation.

Proof: A Function That Depends on \( \overline{z} \) Is Not Analytic

Method 1: Cauchy–Riemann Equations

If \( f(z) = F(z, \overline{z}) \), then it depends on both \( x + iy \) and \( x - iy \).

Analytic functions must satisfy:

$$ \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial v}{\partial y},\quad \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = -\frac{\partial v}{\partial x} $$

But if \( f \) depends on \( \overline{z} \), then these conditions fail.

Example:

  • \( f(z) = \overline{z} = x - iy \)
  • \( u = x \), \( v = -y \)
  • \( u_x = 1 \), \( v_y = 0 \Rightarrow u_x \ne v_y \)

⟹ Cauchy–Riemann equations fail ⟹ \( f \) is not analytic.

Method 2: Wirtinger Derivatives

Use:

$$ \frac{\partial}{\partial z} = \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{\partial}{\partial x} - i \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \right), \quad \frac{\partial}{\partial \overline{z}} = \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{\partial}{\partial x} + i \frac{\partial}{\partial y} \right) $$

A function is analytic if and only if: \[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial \overline{z}} = 0 \]

If \( f \) depends on \( \overline{z} \), this derivative is nonzero ⇒ \( f \) is not analytic.

Harmonic Functions and the Laplacian

A function \( u(x, y) \) is harmonic in a region \( R \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2 \) if it satisfies the Laplace equation:

$$ \nabla^2 u = 0 $$

where \( \nabla^2 \) (del squared) is the Laplacian operator:

$$ \nabla^2 u = \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2} $$

In Complex Analysis:

If \( f(z) = u(x, y) + i v(x, y) \) is analytic, then both \( u \) and \( v \) satisfy:

$$ \nabla^2 u = \nabla^2 v = 0 $$

Conclusion:

Every analytic function has real and imaginary parts that are harmonic — i.e., they satisfy \( \nabla^2 = 0 \).

Harmonic Conjugates

If \( u(x, y) \) is harmonic on a region \( R \), a function \( v(x, y) \) is a harmonic conjugate of \( u \) if:

  • \( v \) is harmonic
  • They satisfy the Cauchy–Riemann equations:

$$ \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial v}{\partial y}, \quad \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = -\frac{\partial v}{\partial x} $$

Example:

Let \( u(x, y) = x^2 - y^2 \), and \( v(x, y) = x \).

  • \( u \) is harmonic: \( \nabla^2 u = 2 - 2 = 0 \)
  • \( v \) is harmonic: \( \nabla^2 v = 0 \)
  • But Cauchy–Riemann fails: \( u_x = 2x \ne v_y = 0 \), \( u_y = -2y \ne -v_x = -1 \)

Conclusion: \( v(x, y) = x \) is not a harmonic conjugate of \( u(x, y) = x^2 - y^2 \).

Examples: Finding Harmonic Conjugates (with Integration)

Example 1: \( u(x, y) = \arctan\left( \frac{y}{x} \right) \)

This function is harmonic in any region excluding the negative real axis (to keep \( \arg(z) \) single-valued).

Step 1: Verify Harmonicity

This is a known harmonic function: \( \nabla^2 u = 0 \) in \( \mathbb{R}^2 \setminus \{ x = 0 \} \).

Step 2: Use Cauchy–Riemann equations

We compute:

$$ u_x = \frac{-y}{x^2 + y^2}, \quad u_y = \frac{x}{x^2 + y^2} $$

Then from C-R:

$$ v_y = u_x = \frac{-y}{x^2 + y^2}, \quad v_x = -u_y = \frac{-x}{x^2 + y^2} $$

Step 3: Integrate
  • Integrate \( v_y = \frac{-y}{x^2 + y^2} \) w.r.t. \( y \):

    • Let’s integrate keeping \( x \) constant:
    \( v(x, y) = \int \frac{-y}{x^2 + y^2} \, dy \)
    Use substitution: \( u = x^2 + y^2 \Rightarrow du = 2y \, dy \)

    So: $$ \int \frac{-y}{x^2 + y^2} \, dy = -\frac{1}{2} \ln(x^2 + y^2) + C(x) $$

  • Final result:

    • $$ v(x, y) = -\frac{1}{2} \ln(x^2 + y^2) + C(x) $$

  • Now verify with \( v_x \), or notice that this matches \( \ln |z| \) up to constants.
Final Answer:

$$ f(z) = \arg(z) + i \ln|z| = \ln z $$

Example 2: \( u(x, y) = e^x \cos y \)

Step 1: Check that \( u \) is harmonic

$$ u_{xx} = e^x \cos y, \quad u_{yy} = -e^x \cos y, \quad \nabla^2 u = u_{xx} + u_{yy} = 0 $$

Step 2: Cauchy–Riemann Equations

$$ v_y = u_x = e^x \cos y, \quad v_x = -u_y = e^x \sin y $$

Step 3: Integrate to find \( v(x, y) \)
  • Start with \( v_y = e^x \cos y \)

    • \( x \) is constant ⇒ $$ v(x, y) = \int e^x \cos y \, dy = e^x \sin y + C(x) $$

  • Now check with \( v_x \):

    • $$ v_x = \frac{\partial}{\partial x} (e^x \sin y + C(x)) = e^x \sin y + C'(x) $$

  • From C-R: \( v_x = e^x \sin y \Rightarrow C'(x) = 0 \Rightarrow C(x) = \text{const} \)
Final Result:

$$ v(x, y) = e^x \sin y \quad \Rightarrow \quad f(z) = u + i v = e^x \cos y + i e^x \sin y = e^z $$

Conclusion:

  • Example 1: \( u = \arg(z) \), harmonic conjugate \( v = \ln|z| \), gives \( f(z) = \ln z \)
  • Example 2: \( u = e^x \cos y \), harmonic conjugate \( v = e^x \sin y \), gives \( f(z) = e^z \)
