Electronics Basics

Well, transistors ‚Äî right? These tiny semiconductors are the starting point of the entire digital evolution! At the core of it all, we have two main types: PNP and NPN.

As shown here, a PNP transistor has current flowing from the emitter to the collector, while the NPN transistor does the opposite ‚Äî current flows from collector to emitter.

In both cases, the base acts like a gate: a small current at the base controls a much larger current through the transistor. That‚Äôs what makes them so powerful ‚Äî they can switch or amplify signals, which is the foundation of all digital electronics.

And that‚Äôs exactly why they‚Äôre called Bipolar Junction Transistors (BJTs) ‚Äî because both electrons and holes are involved in the conduction process.

So what are ‚Äúholes,‚Äù and what do electrons actually do here? We‚Äôll break it down in detail in Physics of Electronics.

Transistor Regions of Operation

From this point on, we‚Äôll refer to the Emitter-Base Junction as EBJ, and the Collector-Base Junction as CBJ.

Each junction can be either in a Forward Bias or Reverse Bias condition. That gives us four possible states, which define the region in which the transistor is operating:

\[ \begin{array}{|c|c|c|} \hline \textbf{Region} & \textbf{EBJ} & \textbf{CBJ} \\ \hline \text{Active} & \text{Forward} & \text{Reverse} \\ \hline \text{Saturation} & \text{Forward} & \text{Forward} \\ \hline \text{Cut-off} & \text{Reverse} & \text{Reverse} \\ \hline \text{Reverse Active} & \text{Reverse} & \text{Forward} \\ \hline \end{array} \]

Out of these four conditions, the Active Mode is used in analog applications ‚Äî like amplifiers ‚Äî where the transistor operates in its linear region.

On the other hand, Cut-off and Saturation are used in digital electronics, where the transistor functions as a switch ‚Äî either fully OFF or fully ON.

How the Three Layers Work Together

A Bipolar Junction Transistor (BJT) is built from three layers of semiconductor material, forming two p-n junctions:

  • Emitter (E) ‚Äî heavily doped, supplies charge carriers
  • Base (B) ‚Äî very thin and lightly doped
  • Collector (C) ‚Äî moderately doped, collects carriers

When properly biased in active mode, the transistor acts like a dependent current source. That means the output current (collector current) is controlled by the input current (base current).

Reverse Bias on the Collector-Base Junction (CBJ)

In active mode, the collector-base junction (CBJ) is reverse-biased. The collector is at a higher potential than the base, which keeps this junction in reverse bias.

What we see here is a collector-base junction of a transistor, using a collector resistor \( R_C \) and a supply voltage \( V_{CC} \). This setup puts the CBJ in reverse bias ‚Äî exactly what‚Äôs required for active mode.

Behind the scenes, there‚Äôs constant interaction between electrons and holes. If we inject an electron carrier into the base, that motion contributes to current ‚Äî and what we observe externally is the result of these carrier movements at the junction.

This shows what‚Äôs happening at the collector-base junction (CBJ) in reverse bias. Electrons that made it through the base are pulled into the collector, driven by the electric field across the CBJ. This movement creates the collector current ‚Äî a key part of active mode operation.

Active Mode: Everything in Place

In this setup, we‚Äôre fully in active mode. The emitter-base junction (EBJ) is forward-biased, and the collector-base junction (CBJ) is reverse-biased.

Electrons are injected from the emitter, pass through the base, and are swept into the collector. This is how we finally create the collector current \( I_C \).

What‚Äôs interesting now is that \( I_C \) becomes dependent on the voltage across the base-emitter junction: \( I_C = f(V_{BE}) \). Change \( V_{BE} \), and you control the output ‚Äî that‚Äôs the transistor as a current amplifier.
