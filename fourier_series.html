Fourier Series

Think of a Fourier series like building a funky dance-party playlist out of pure beats:

  • Your song (the wiggly curve): Any repeating pattern‚Äîlike the beat of a drum or the curve of a roller-coaster track.
  • Pure notes (sine & cosine): Two super simple sounds: a smooth up-and-down wave (sin) and a smooth side-to-side wave (cos).
  • Mixing harmonics: Speed those waves up‚Äî2√ó faster, 3√ó faster, etc. Each is a ‚Äúharmonic,‚Äù like adding faster drum rolls to your basic beat.
  • Putting it all together: Stack a bit of each wave‚Äîsome slow, some fast‚Äîuntil the pile of waves wiggles exactly like your original pattern.

Mathematically

You pick a fundamental ‚Äústep‚Äù (frequency) that matches your pattern‚Äôs repeat rate. For each harmonic n, you choose two volumes (coefficients) an (cosine) and bn (sine).

The sum

$$f(x) \;\approx\; \frac{a_0}{2} \;+\;\sum_{n=1}^\infty\Bigl[a_n\cos(n\omega_0 x)\;+\;b_n\sin(n\omega_0 x)\Bigr]$$

Why it‚Äôs cool

  • Break any beat or bump into a recipe of pure waves.
  • In music, isolate bass, mids, and trebles.
  • In engineering, solve heat, sound, and light problems by working with simple waves.

Derivation of Fourier Series Formulas using Euler‚Äôs Formula

Introduction

A Fourier series is an expansion of a periodic function \(f(x)\) with period \(T\) (i.e., \(f(x+T)=f(x)\)) into a sum of sines and cosines, or equivalently into complex exponentials. We will use Euler‚Äôs formula as a cornerstone for these derivations. The angular frequency is \(\omega=\frac{2\pi}{T}\). Often, the period is taken as \(2L\), in which case \(T=2L\) and \(\omega=\frac{\pi}{L}\). We will use period \(T\) and angular frequency \(\omega\).

Euler‚Äôs Formula and its Consequences

Definition (Euler‚Äôs Formula)

For any real \(\theta\), Euler‚Äôs formula states:

\[ e^{i\theta} \;=\; \cos(\theta) \;+\; i\,\sin(\theta) \]

where \(i^2=-1\).

Lemma (Trigonometric Functions from Euler‚Äôs Formula)

\[ \cos(\theta)=\frac{e^{i\theta}+e^{-i\theta}}{2}, \quad \sin(\theta)=\frac{e^{i\theta}-e^{-i\theta}}{2i}. \]

Orthogonality Relations

The derivation of Fourier coefficients relies on the orthogonality of sine, cosine, and complex exponential functions over one period. Let \(n,m\) be integers. We integrate over \([0,T]\), with \(\omega=2\pi/T\).

Lemma (Orthogonality of Sines and Cosines)

  1. \(\displaystyle\int_0^T\cos(n\omega x)\,dx =\begin{cases}T,&n=0,\\0,&n\neq0.\end{cases}\)
  2. \(\displaystyle\int_0^T\sin(n\omega x)\,dx=0\) for all \(n\).
  3. \(\displaystyle\int_0^T\cos(n\omega x)\cos(m\omega x)\,dx =\begin{cases}T/2,&n=m\neq0,\\0,&n\neq m.\end{cases}\)
  4. \(\displaystyle\int_0^T\sin(n\omega x)\sin(m\omega x)\,dx =\begin{cases}T/2,&n=m\neq0,\\0,&n\neq m.\end{cases}\)
  5. \(\displaystyle\int_0^T\sin(n\omega x)\cos(m\omega x)\,dx=0\) for all \(n,m\).

Lemma (Orthogonality of Complex Exponentials)

\[ \int_0^T e^{i n\omega x}\,e^{-i m\omega x}\,dx =\int_0^T e^{i(n-m)\omega x}\,dx =\begin{cases}T,&n=m,\\0,&n\neq m.\end{cases} \]

Trigonometric Fourier Series

Any \(T\)-periodic \(f(x)\) admits the expansion

\[ f(x) =\frac{a_0}{2} +\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\bigl[a_n\cos(n\omega x)+b_n\sin(n\omega x)\bigr]. \]

Coefficients

\[ a_0=\frac{2}{T}\int_0^T f(x)\,dx, \quad a_n=\frac{2}{T}\int_0^T f(x)\cos(n\omega x)\,dx,\;(n\ge1), \quad b_n=\frac{2}{T}\int_0^T f(x)\sin(n\omega x)\,dx,\;(n\ge1). \]

Complex Exponential Fourier Series

The same function can be written as

\[ f(x) =\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}c_n\,e^{i n\omega x}, \quad c_n=\frac{1}{T}\int_0^T f(x)\,e^{-i n\omega x}\,dx. \]

Relationship of Coefficients

\[ c_0=\frac{a_0}{2},\quad c_n=\frac{a_n - i b_n}{2},\quad c_{-n}=\frac{a_n + i b_n}{2}, \]

and conversely \(\;a_n=c_n+c_{-n},\;b_n=i(c_n-c_{-n})\).

Conclusion

We have derived both the trigonometric and complex Fourier series expansions, relying on Euler‚Äôs formula and orthogonality over one period.

Understanding the Relationship Between Real and Complex Coefficients

Why do we get the identities:

\[ c_n = \frac{1}{2}(a_n - ib_n), \quad c_{-n} = \frac{1}{2}(a_n + ib_n) \]

These formulas arise when we rewrite the real Fourier series using Euler‚Äôs formula into its complex exponential form. Let's walk through the derivation conceptually and mathematically.

From Real to Complex Fourier Series

Start from the real Fourier series:

\[ f(x) = \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^\infty \left[a_n \cos(nx) + b_n \sin(nx)\right] \]

Apply Euler's identities:

  • \(\cos(nx) = \frac{e^{inx} + e^{-inx}}{2}\)
  • \(\sin(nx) = \frac{e^{inx} - e^{-inx}}{2i}\)

Plug these into the series:

\[ \begin{aligned} f(x) &= \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^\infty \left[ a_n \cdot \frac{e^{inx} + e^{-inx}}{2} + b_n \cdot \frac{e^{inx} - e^{-inx}}{2i} \right] \\ &= \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^\infty \left[ \left(\frac{a_n}{2} - \frac{b_n}{2i} \right)e^{inx} + \left(\frac{a_n}{2} + \frac{b_n}{2i} \right)e^{-inx} \right] \end{aligned} \]

Define the complex coefficients:

\[ c_n = \frac{1}{2}(a_n - ib_n), \quad c_{-n} = \frac{1}{2}(a_n + ib_n) \]

This transforms the series into the complex form:

\[ f(x) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty c_n e^{inx} + c_{-n} e^{-inx} + \frac{a_0}{2} \]

Which is equivalent to the full complex Fourier series:

\[ f(x) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} c_n e^{inx} \]

Why These Relationships Work

The real Fourier basis (\(\cos(nx), \sin(nx)\)) and the complex exponential basis (\(e^{inx}\)) are mathematically connected via Euler‚Äôs identities. Expressing one set of basis functions in terms of the other naturally introduces linear combinations of the real coefficients.

Thus, when moving from real coefficients \((a_n, b_n)\) to complex coefficients \(c_n\), you end up combining them into the formulas above, aligning the real and imaginary parts with cosine and sine components.

Analogy

This is analogous to changing coordinate systems. In a Cartesian basis, you use x and y axes separately (cosine and sine). In a rotated complex basis, coordinates become combinations of x and y ‚Äî hence the \(a_n \pm i b_n\) combinations.

Optional: Derivation via Integration

These expressions can also be derived directly from the orthogonality of \(e^{inx}\) over one period, by integrating \(f(x)\cdot e^{-inx}\) and solving. The result leads to the same formulas for \(c_n\) in terms of \(a_n\) and \(b_n\).
