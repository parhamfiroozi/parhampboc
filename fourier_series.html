Fourier Series

Think of a Fourier series like building a funky dance-party playlist out of pure beats:

  • Your song (the wiggly curve): Any repeating pattern—like the beat of a drum or the curve of a roller-coaster track.
  • Pure notes (sine & cosine): Two super simple sounds: a smooth up-and-down wave (sin) and a smooth side-to-side wave (cos).
  • Mixing harmonics: Speed those waves up—2× faster, 3× faster, etc. Each is a “harmonic,” like adding faster drum rolls to your basic beat.
  • Putting it all together: Stack a bit of each wave—some slow, some fast—until the pile of waves wiggles exactly like your original pattern.

Mathematically

You pick a fundamental “step” (frequency) that matches your pattern’s repeat rate. For each harmonic n, you choose two volumes (coefficients) an (cosine) and bn (sine).

The sum

$$f(x) \;\approx\; \frac{a_0}{2} \;+\;\sum_{n=1}^\infty\Bigl[a_n\cos(n\omega_0 x)\;+\;b_n\sin(n\omega_0 x)\Bigr]$$

Why it’s cool

  • Break any beat or bump into a recipe of pure waves.
  • In music, isolate bass, mids, and trebles.
  • In engineering, solve heat, sound, and light problems by working with simple waves.

Derivation of Fourier Coefficients (Euler’s Formulas)

1. Assumptions & Setup

Let f(x) be periodic with period 2L (i.e. f(x + 2L)=f(x)). Assume it admits the expansion:

f(x) = a₀⁄2 + Σn=1∞ [aₙ cos(nπx ⁄ L) + bₙ sin(nπx ⁄ L)]

2. Orthogonality Lemmas

  • ∫−LL cos(nπx ⁄ L) dx = 0
  • ∫−LL sin(nπx ⁄ L) dx = 0
  • ∫−LL sin(mπx ⁄ L) cos(nπx ⁄ L) dx = 0
  • ∫−LL cos(mπx ⁄ L) cos(nπx ⁄ L) dx = {0 if m≠n; L if m=n≠0}
  • ∫−LL sin(mπx ⁄ L) sin(nπx ⁄ L) dx = {0 if m≠n; L if m=n≠0}

3. Formula for a₀

Integrate over [−L,L], use Lemmas 1–2:

∫−LLf(x) dx = a₀ L ⇒ a₀ = (1⁄L) ∫−LLf(x) dx

4. Formula for aₙ (n ≥ 1)

Multiply by cos(nπx⁄L), integrate, use Lemmas 3–4:

∫−LLf(x) cos(nπx⁄L) dx = aₙ L ⇒ aₙ = (1⁄L) ∫−LLf(x) cos(nπx⁄L) dx

5. Formula for bₙ (n ≥ 1)

Multiply by sin(nπx⁄L), integrate, use Lemmas 3–5:

∫−LLf(x) sin(nπx⁄L) dx = bₙ L ⇒ bₙ = (1⁄L) ∫−LLf(x) sin(nπx⁄L) dx

6. Dirichlet’s Convergence Conditions

The series converges to f(x) (or to the midpoint at jumps) if:
• f is 2L-periodic;
• f is piecewise continuous on [−L,L];
• f has finitely many extrema and discontinuities there.
